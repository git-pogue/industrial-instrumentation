Overview

What is Industrial Instrumentation?

Industrial instrumentation is the science and practice of measuring, monitoring, and controlling physical and chemical variables within industrial processes. Typical measurements include pressure, temperature, flow, level, and analytical properties (e.g., pH, conductivity, gas composition). Instruments convert real-world signals into usable information for operators and control systems (PLC/DCS), enabling safe, efficient, and compliant operations.

Pressure

Gauges and transmitters detect fluid pressure using mechanisms like Bourdon tubes, diaphragms, and piezo-resistive/strain sensors. Outputs often 4–20 mA or digital.

Temperature

Thermocouples, RTDs, and infrared sensors measure thermal energy; transmitters linearize and scale signals for control and monitoring.

Flow

Differential pressure, electromagnetic, Coriolis, vortex, ultrasonic, and turbine meters quantify volumetric or mass flow rates of liquids and gases.

flow.htmlExplore Flow →

Level

Hydrostatic, radar, ultrasonic, capacitance, and float-based devices determine tank and vessel level—both continuous and point detection.

level.htmlExplore Level →

Analytical

Sensors analyze chemical or gas properties—pH/ORP, conductivity, dissolved oxygen, turbidity, and gas detection (electrochemical/NDIR).

Signal & Control: Instruments output analog (e.g., 4–20 mA, HART) or digital (Modbus, Profibus, FOUNDATION Fieldbus, Ethernet/IP). Controllers and safety systems use these signals for PID loops, alarming, and interlocks.
